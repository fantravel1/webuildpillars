Digital Sovereignty for the First Peoples

For millennia, Indigenous peoples have been the stewards of knowledge, culture, and land. Today, a new territory demands attention: the digital realm. And just as colonizers claimed physical land, the most valuable digital real estate has been claimed by those with capital—not cultural authority.

Indigenous communities face unique challenges in the digital space. Many reservations and remote communities lack reliable internet access. Tribal nations, despite their sovereignty, often lack resources for premium digital infrastructure. Indigenous languages, art forms, and traditional knowledge risk being lost when communities can't establish authoritative online presence.

Meanwhile, premium domains containing Indigenous cultural terms are often owned by non-Indigenous speculators. We're working to change that.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can tribal nations get free premium domains?

Yes. Federally recognized tribes, state-recognized tribes, First Nations bands, and Indigenous governing bodies worldwide can apply for premium domains. We honor tribal sovereignty and work with Indigenous communities on their own terms.

Do you serve Indigenous peoples outside the US?

Absolutely. We serve Indigenous peoples globally, including First Nations in Canada, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples in Australia, Māori in New Zealand, and Indigenous communities throughout the Americas, Africa, Asia, and the Pacific.

What about individual Indigenous artists?

Yes. Individual Indigenous artists, craftspeople, musicians, and creators can apply. You don't need to be a tribal entity or registered nonprofit—we support individual creators whose work preserves and promotes Indigenous culture.

Do you have domains with Indigenous language words?

Our portfolio focuses primarily on English-language domains, but we're open to discussing Indigenous language domains on a case-by-case basis. We're also committed to never holding domains containing Indigenous cultural terms for speculation.
