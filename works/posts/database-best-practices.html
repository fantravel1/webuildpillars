Best Practices for Databases

Data management fundamentals for organizations building digital archives.

Your organization's data is one of its most valuable assets. Whether you're managing donor records, community archives, or creative portfolios, how you structure and protect that data matters.

Start with Purpose

Before choosing a database, answer these questions:

  • What data are you collecting? Contacts, donations, content, member info?
  • Who needs access? Staff, volunteers, the public?
  • How will you use it? Reporting, outreach, archiving, analysis?
  • What's your growth trajectory? 100 records or 100,000?

Common Database Types

CRM (Customer Relationship Management)

For managing relationships with donors, members, or clients. Popular options for nonprofits:

  • Salesforce Nonprofit: Powerful but complex, free for qualifying nonprofits
  • Bloomerang: Donor-focused, easier learning curve
  • Airtable: Flexible, good for smaller organizations

Content Management Databases

For managing articles, media, or creative works. Most CMS platforms (WordPress, etc.) have built-in databases. For larger archives:

  • Omeka: Built for cultural heritage archives
  • CollectiveAccess: For museums and archives

Spreadsheets (When Appropriate)

Sometimes a well-organized spreadsheet is enough. Use spreadsheets when:

  • You have fewer than 1,000 records
  • You don't need complex relationships between data
  • You have limited technical capacity
  • You're just starting out

Core Best Practices

1. Data Hygiene

  • Consistent formatting: Decide on standards (dates, phone numbers, addresses) and enforce them
  • Regular cleaning: Remove duplicates, update outdated info quarterly
  • Required fields: Define what information is essential vs. optional

2. Security and Privacy

  • Access control: Limit who can view/edit sensitive data
  • Encryption: Protect data in transit and at rest
  • Privacy compliance: Know your obligations (GDPR, state laws)
  • Data minimization: Only collect what you actually need

3. Backup and Recovery

  • Regular backups: Automated, off-site, tested regularly
  • Version history: Ability to recover from mistakes
  • Disaster plan: Know how you'd recover if everything failed

4. Documentation

  • Data dictionary: Document what each field means and how it's used
  • Process documentation: How data is entered, cleaned, and maintained
  • Access logs: Know who accessed what and when

For Knowledge Builders: Archive Considerations

If you're preserving cultural or historical content, additional considerations apply:

  • Metadata standards: Use established standards (Dublin Core, etc.) for discoverability
  • File formats: Prefer open, non-proprietary formats for long-term access
  • Provenance: Document where materials came from and chain of custody
  • Rights management: Track copyright, permissions, cultural sensitivities
