Preserving Stories That Matter

Community archives preserve the histories that mainstream institutions often overlook. The stories of neighborhoods, cultural groups, and marginalized communities need permanent digital homes.

A professional domain like YourArchive.org ensures that your preservation work has a stable, credible address that researchers, community members, and future generations can find and trust.

We Build Pillars gifts premium domains—valued at $500 to $100,000+—to community archive projects. Whether you're digitizing photographs, recording oral histories, or building a neighborhood memory project, we want to help you build on solid digital ground.

Why Permanence Matters for Archives

Archives are about the long term. The domain you choose today needs to work for decades. Consider:

  • Link permanence — Researchers cite your URLs. They need to work in 20 years.
  • Institutional memory — Staff changes, but the domain remains.
  • Trust building — A professional domain signals seriousness to funders and partners.
  • Discoverability — Short, memorable domains get found and shared.

Free subdomains on platform sites can disappear when companies change or fold. A domain you own is infrastructure you control.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can community archives get free domains?

Yes. We Build Pillars gifts premium domains valued at $500 to $100,000+ to community archive projects. A professional domain gives your archive a permanent address that researchers, community members, and the public can rely on for years to come.

What kinds of archives qualify?

We support community-based archives, oral history projects, cultural preservation initiatives, local history collections, neighborhood documentation projects, and digital humanities initiatives that preserve the stories of underrepresented communities.

Do we need to be a formal institution?

No. We support grassroots archive projects, independent preservationists, and community groups alongside formal historical societies and institutions. The focus is on the preservation work, not organizational structure.
